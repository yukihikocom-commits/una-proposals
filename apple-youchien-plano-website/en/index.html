Apple Youchien Plano (あっぷる幼稚園 プレイノ園) is a Japanese-language bilingual preschool in Plano, TX 75025, serving children ages 2.5 to 5 across Plano, Frisco, McKinney, Allen, Prosper and the Dallas area. About

A home away from home, now in Texas.

“Let all that you do be done in love.” (1 Corinthians 16:14)

Apple Youchien began in San Diego, California in 2005. Guided by Christian love and Steiner education, we have kept a warm, home-like practice that follows the pace of each child.

In October 2025 we received our Texas license and opened our third campus in Plano — a Japanese bilingual preschool where children living in America can spend their days inside the Japanese language, its seasons, and its traditions.

Our teachers hold early childhood credentials from both Japan and the United States. In mixed-age classes for ages 2½ to 5, older children naturally learn to look after the younger ones.

Our approach

Language, handwork, and living things.

We do not ask children to memorize. We let them feel first, with their hands and their whole bodies. Three threads run through every day at our Japanese immersion preschool.

Japanese, through the year

Hinamatsuri, Children's Day, Tanabata, the Christmas program. Living in America, children still meet the Japanese calendar and the Japanese language all year long. Children who are not yet comfortable in Japanese join our gentle Japanese childcare time; children who find English difficult have English play during extended care. Language takes root slowly, through songs and repetition.

Handwork of the season

Each season we hold a handwork gathering using natural materials, including plant dyeing. We start by gathering onion skins, tie the cloth, lower it into the dye bath, and watch the color turn together. What matters is not only the finished cloth but the process of making it. Families are sometimes invited to join.

The garden and the kitchen

The Apple Garden is a small plot where we grow seasonal flowers and vegetables. Tending it, children touch soil, water, and light, and come to know the feel of caring for something alive. What they grow goes into our monthly cooking, which they prepare and eat themselves. Respect for food travels through the hands before it reaches words.

Four seasons

The year turns through color.

There is a season table in the classroom and a garden outside. Our festivals are not there to mark the calendar — they are there so children can meet a color and a texture that belongs to that season alone.

  1. Spring

    Hinamatsuri and Children's Day. We make chirashi sushi and craft hina dolls. Seeds go into the Apple Garden.

  2. Summer

    Tanabata. Wishes are written on paper strips and tied to bamboo. The garden fills, and its vegetables become our monthly cooking.

  3. Autumn

    The harvest. Hands go into the soil, and onion skins become plant dye. The moment the color turns, the room fills with cheers.

  4. Winter

    Songs at the Christmas program. Then ozoni, first calligraphy, and New Year games. The year closes and opens in a Japanese shape.

A sample of our festivals. Programs vary by year and by campus.
